Sous Chef Stove

4 weeks | Student Project

UX Research, Branding, Wireframes, Usability Testing, Visual Design, Prototype

A Smart Kitchen Assistant

Smart technology innovations are turning the dream of the smart home into reality. This design challenge explores how a smart stove can impact everyday problems. As a second part to the challenge—I designed a companion app that expands the stove’s capabilities and impact. Even after this completing exercise, I have barely scraped the surface of what a smart stove can do. This concept is just the beginning, and that is exciting.

Seeing the Potential

Samsung’s smart refrigerator Family Hub made its debut in Spring 2016. Featuring a built-in touchscreen and camera, Family Hub organizes home tasks, connects family members, and provides entertainment. Family Hub is not just a high-end refrigerator with technologically advanced features, but as its name suggests, it acts as a medium to connect family members as well as a kitchen assistant. It places itself at the center of family activity.

As a design challenge, I wanted to design another appliance to add to Samsung’s line of smart home appliances. Like Family Hub, its impact would apply to everyday lives and be integrated into the home. No longer just an appliance but an assistant in our daily routines.

Why a Stove?

I considered a microwave, washer, dryer, lamp, TV and even a bathroom mirror. I ended up choosing an appliance that would create the most impact in everyday life---the stove. The most complex and regularly occurring tasks happen around the stove. I started thinking about a smart stove could do. Create a more enjoyable cooking experience? Yes! Preheat our oven remotely? Definitely! What were the less obvious problems we can solve with this stove? The potential was exciting to contemplate, dream about and explore.

Uncover Problems
Before Solutions

When you’re bringing something new to the table, it needs to be boldly innovative yet delightfully relevant. Realizing that I would be crossing the line from “need” to “want”, I was targeting a niche market. I started thinking about who would want this stove? What problems do they have? What would get them to not only use the stove but love it?

I interviewed 7 individuals varying in income, age, cooking skill level, family size, and tech enthusiasm. Building and refining my hypotheses with each interview, I gained insight to their routines, their daily obstacles, their frustrations in their cooking experiences, and their motivations and obstacles to purchasing a smart stove. I also discussed potential features and learned which they were interested in seeing in a smart stove. Finally, I conducted a mass survey to back my findings.

We Found Our Target

Sous Chef’s target consumers were early tech adopters who desired more precision or assistance in their cooking process and tasks. They all belonged in the mid to high income bracket. The target consumers broke down into three main groups:

Advanced Chefs

Features that enhance their cooking process

Precision + quality

Busy Parents

Make dinner & getting out the door quick and easy

Safety + efficiency

Boomers

Safety features & reminders

Safety + assistance

Their reasons for wanting extra assistance varied. The three main motivating factors were Safety, Precision, and Productivity. These goals drove the design of the smart stove.

Safety

This was the biggest obstacle and yet the strongest motivating factor at the same time. Having the stove complete tasks for you without supervision sounded scary to some. Forget about a smart gas stove. That’s for sure! However, if the stove proved to be even safer than your stove at home, this was where consumers were more willing to pay a premium for a smart stove.

Precision

Cooking enthusiasts were most interested in controlling temperature with precision and guidance in managing time more effectively. Again, they don’t want the stove do it for them but rather help them manage time or temperature so that they can focus on building flavor.

Productivity

Finding time to satisfy the demands of modern life is quite a challenge for many users. Cutting down the number of steps and enabling users to accomplish tasks more quickly would greatly improve their quality of life.

Getting Right Into It

As Sous Chef was breaking new ground, I wanted to get ideas down fast and get to testing early in the process. The project scope focused on the stove’s built-in screen’s UI. However, I did conclude that the stovetop had to have induction burners for anyone to feel safe using the smart features. Next to the screen, users can control burners with touch dials and power buttons as quickly as they would on a traditional stove.

User Flow

I drew up user stories based off the goals of our target consumers and formed a user flow map to identify all the decision making points.

Wireframes

From the insight gleaned from research, I landed on four smart features for the MVP:

Timed Cooking

Sous Chef adds more value to the good old kitchen timer by assigning it to a burner and adding an action after the timer goes off.

Temperature Control

With a clip-on thermometer connected to Sous Chef via wi-fi, stovetop temperature control cooking methods like sous-vide and deep fry are possible without extra appliances taking up counter space.

Automated Tasks

Sous Chef can perform regularly occurring tasks on a repeating schedule.

Safety

Sous Chef's cooktop surface senses when something boils over and turns down the heat.

Observations

Assistance Over Automation

Cooking enthusiasts want more control over their cooking rather than less. They wanted an assistant that enabled them as the “executive chef” versus a robot that took over the entire cooking process.

Points of Stress

The most stressful times of the day occur when trying to get out the door in the morning and getting dinner ready on time. The features of Sous Chef largely focused on alleviating these points of stress.

Safety First

Sous Chef had to prove to be safe first before consumers would consider any of its smart features. Parents with young kids and adults with elderly parents especially are safety-conscious. In fact, safety was the strongest motivator for paying the premium for a smart stove.

Task Management

Managing the stove, following a recipe, and meal planning while trying to watch young children and complete house chores…modern life gets busy. People are looking for ways complete tasks more easily and increase control over their time. Many use timers while they multitask, and sometimes forget which timer was set for which task.

Routine Opportunities

There is a lot of water boiling and toasting happening in the morning. I aimed to cut down the number of steps in morning routines and get the user out the door more quickly.
